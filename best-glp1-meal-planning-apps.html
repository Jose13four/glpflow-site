We tested 7 popular meal planning and food tracking apps against GLP-1-specific needs. Here's what works, what doesn't, and when to use each one.

What to Look for in a GLP-1 Meal Planning App

Most meal planning apps were designed for the general population — people aiming for 2000 calories and balanced macros. But you're not eating 2000 calories. You're on a medication that's literally designed to make you eat less. Your needs are different.

When comparing apps for GLP-1 use, look for these features:

Protein Tracking

Every bite on GLP-1 needs to count. You're eating maybe 1200-1600 calories with a much smaller appetite, which means your macro targets are totally different from someone eating 2000 calories. You need 25-35g protein per meal minimum. Apps that make macro targets customizable (not just calorie counters) are essential.

Small Portion Recipes

Most recipe databases are built around standard serving sizes. A "chicken breast" recipe might feed 2-3 people, but on Ozempic you eat a third of a chicken breast. Apps with built-in scaling and portion control make a real difference in usability. Even better: apps that include recipes specifically designed for smaller appetites.

Injection Cycle Awareness

Your appetite and nausea aren't constant throughout the week. Days 1-2 after injection are brutal; days 5-7 are much easier. The best apps for GLP-1 acknowledge this with rotating meal suggestions or nausea-friendly meal options. Standard apps treat every day the same.

Custom Macro Targets

A GLP-1-specific app lets you set your own targets: maybe 120g protein, 80g fat, 100g carbs on 1200 calories. A calorie-first app forces you into standard ratios that don't match your needs. Flexibility matters.

Key tip: The best app for you depends on whether you want structure (meal planning) or flexibility (food logging). Meal planning saves decision fatigue. Food logging gives you freedom but requires discipline. Some people use both: a planner for weekdays and logging for weekends.

The 7 Best Meal Planning Apps for GLP-1 Users

1. Noom

$40-60/month
Best for: Behavioral psychology approach to weight loss (not specific to GLP-1).

Noom is built around habit change and psychology, not medication. It categorizes foods as "green" (low calorie), "yellow" (moderate), and "red" (calorie-dense). The app includes daily lessons and coaching, which appeals to people who want the "why" behind their food choices.

What it does well: The behavior-first approach is refreshing if you struggle with emotional eating. The food database is solid, and the app combines logging with psychological insights. If you enjoy learning-based approaches, the daily lessons make weight loss feel less like deprivation.

What it lacks: Noom doesn't account for GLP-1 users at all. It'll flag your natural smaller portions as undereating. It's calorie-focused (red/yellow/green system), not macro-focused, so protein tracking takes a backseat. At $40-60/month, it's expensive for what you're getting if your main need is meal logging.

Bottom line: Good app, wrong context. If you like behavioral coaching, it works, but you'll need separate guidance on GLP-1-specific nutrition.

2. MyFitnessPal

Free + Premium $70/year
Best for: Detailed macro tracking and food database accuracy.

MyFitnessPal has the largest food database of any app — over 14 million entries crowdsourced from users. You can log almost anything and get accurate protein, carb, fat, and calorie breakdowns. It's the industry standard for people who care about macros.

What it does well: The food database is unmatched. Searching for "Greek yogurt, 2% full fat, plain" returns dozens of options with slightly different macros, so you can find your exact brand. Macro tracking is extremely detailed. The free tier is genuinely useful — logging, barcode scanning, basic macro targets. The app syncs with fitness trackers and other health apps.

What it lacks: MyFitnessPal is calorie-first. It's not GLP-1-aware. You'll get notifications that you're "undereating" on days when you're eating exactly the right amount for semaglutide. It doesn't suggest GLP-1-friendly meals or account for injection cycles. No meal planning features — just logging. Premium costs $70/year and mostly adds useless features; the free tier is where the value is.

Bottom line: Best app for logging macros accurately. Use it as a tool, not as your primary GLP-1 guide. Pair it with GLP-1-specific meal ideas from elsewhere.

3. Lose It!

Free + Premium $90/year
Best for: Simple calorie tracking with minimal friction.

Lose It! is intentionally stripped-down. It focuses on calorie counting without the psychology lessons, behavior tracking, or premium upsells. The interface is clean, fast, and doesn't overwhelm you with features you don't need.

What it does well: The user interface is genuinely good — minimal, fast, and intuitive. Barcode scanning works smoothly. The free tier is actually useful and doesn't constantly nag you to upgrade. If you just want to log calories and move on, Lose It! gets out of your way.

What it lacks: Like most general apps, it's calorie-focused, not macro-focused. Protein tracking is possible but requires manual setup. No GLP-1-specific features. No meal planning. Premium ($90/year) adds macro tracking, which moves it closer to MyFitnessPal territory — but MyFitnessPal free is still better for macros.

Bottom line: Best app if you just want simple calorie logging without bells and whistles. Works fine on GLP-1, but you'll need to get macro targets and meal ideas elsewhere.

4. PlateJoy

$8-15/month (subscription)
Best for: Weekly meal planning with shopping lists and custom macros.

PlateJoy is a meal planning engine that generates weekly menus based on your dietary preferences (keto, paleo, vegan, etc.), macro targets, and dislikes. It includes shopping lists and syncs recipes to your phone. You get structure without having to decide what to eat.

What it does well: Meal planning removes decision fatigue. You get a full week of menus + shopping list every Sunday, which saves real time. You can customize macro targets and dietary restrictions (gluten-free, dairy-free, etc.). The recipes are from real sources (Food Network, Bon Appetit, etc.), so they taste good, not "diet food." The app adapts if you don't like a meal — it learns your preferences.

What it lacks: PlateJoy uses "standard" portion sizes, so a chicken breast is still a full chicken breast. You'll need to manually cut recipes down for GLP-1 portions. No GLP-1-specific guidance or injection cycle awareness. The subscription is required — no free trial beyond the first meal plan.

Bottom line: Best meal planning app overall. If you struggle with decision fatigue and want a structured weekly plan, PlateJoy saves money on food waste and time on meal decisions. Scale the portions down for GLP-1.

5. EatLove

$15-20/month
Best for: Dietitian-created meal plans with personalized macro targets.

EatLove pairs AI meal planning with licensed dietitian support. You fill out a health profile, and the app generates personalized weekly meal plans. You also get access to real dietitians for questions. It's a hybrid between an automated planner and professional nutrition guidance.

What it does well: The dietitian element is valuable — real human guidance, not just algorithms. The meal plans include macros and are nutritionally balanced. You can message dietitians with questions. It's more personalized than PlateJoy because a human has reviewed your profile.

What it lacks: No GLP-1-specific profiles or meal planning. The dietitians may not specialize in semaglutide. Portions are still standard size — you'll scale them down. The app is newer than competitors, so the recipe database is smaller. Premium cost ($15-20/month) is higher than PlateJoy.

Bottom line: Good if you want human dietitian input. But if your dietitian isn't GLP-1-specialized, you'll get generic guidance that doesn't account for your appetite and nausea patterns.

6. Yummly

Free + Premium $120/year
Best for: Recipe discovery and inspiration (not meal planning or tracking).

Yummly is a recipe search engine with AI that learns your tastes. You rate recipes, and the app gets better at recommending dishes you'll actually want to cook. It's primarily a discovery tool, not a meal planning or tracking app.

What it does well: The recipe discovery is genuinely smart. The more you use it, the better it gets at suggesting recipes you'll like. The database is huge (millions of recipes). You can filter by dietary preferences, prep time, and skill level. It integrates with grocery delivery services for one-click shopping lists.

What it lacks: Yummly isn't a meal planner or food tracker. It doesn't generate weekly menus or track calories. No macro tracking. No nutritional guidance. It's purely recipe inspiration. For GLP-1 users specifically, it can help you find small-portion or high-protein recipes, but you need to do the legwork of searching and filtering.

Bottom line: Great if you like cooking and want inspiration. Not helpful if you need structure or macro tracking. Use it alongside a logging or planning app, not instead of one.

FREE (Newsletter)
Best for: GLP-1-specific meal ideas, injection cycle guidance, and community.

GLP Flow is a free weekly newsletter + resource site designed specifically for people on semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound). Each week, you get high-protein meal ideas, portion guidance, and tactics for different phases of your injection cycle. No app fees. No subscription tricks.

It acknowledges nausea, injection cycles, and the emotional side of eating less. The tone is honest and non-judgmental. It's free. You can use the meal ideas with any logging app (MyFitnessPal, Lose It!) to track your macros.

What it lacks: GLP Flow isn't a full app. There's no calorie logging, no barcode scanning, no integration with fitness trackers. You'll still need a separate app for detailed macro tracking if that's important to you. It's newsletter-based, not an app you check constantly.

Bottom line: Best free option if you need GLP-1-specific guidance. Not a replacement for a full tracking or planning app, but excellent as a starting point or foundation. Many users combine GLP Flow's meal ideas with MyFitnessPal or PlateJoy for macro tracking and portion scaling.

What None of These Apps Do Well

Here's the honest part: even the best apps miss critical GLP-1 issues.

Nausea awareness

None of these apps know when you're in the peak nausea phase of your injection cycle. They can't suggest cold foods on day 2 or switch to heartier meals on day 6. You have to manually remember and adapt. GLP Flow tries to address this in the newsletter, but it's still on you to apply it.

Injection day timing

If you inject Thursday night, your appetite is lowest Friday and Saturday. By Wednesday, it's much better. No app knows your injection schedule and adapts recommendations. You're managing this yourself.

GLP-1 nausea foods

Foods that trigger nausea are personal. Heavy oils, greasy meats, and dense carbs cause issues for some people but not others. Apps can't learn your specific triggers well because they're not built for it. You have to log and notice patterns yourself.

Muscle preservation tracking

None of these apps track body composition or muscle loss, which is critical on GLP-1. You could be losing 40% muscle and 60% fat, and a calorie-tracking app won't tell you. Protein macros help, but they're not enough. You need a different tool (scales that measure body composition, or a coach) to monitor this.

The mental game

Eating 1200 calories when apps say 1500 is the minimum for women can make you feel like you're doing it wrong. Apps aren't designed to validate that smaller portions are OK on semaglutide. GLP Flow helps here, but none of the big apps acknowledge this psychological piece.

The best approach: Use an app as a tool, not as your primary source of truth. Combine it with GLP-1-specific guidance (from your doctor, a GLP-1-specialized dietitian, or communities like GLP Flow's newsletter) to fill the gaps.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I look for in a meal planning app for GLP-1?

The best GLP-1 meal planning app tracks protein (25-35g per meal is ideal), includes small portion recipes, accounts for injection cycle fluctuations, and allows custom macro targets. Look for apps that make meal logging easy but don't shame you for eating less — GLP-1 means naturally smaller portions, which some calorie-focused apps misinterpret as undereating.

Is MyFitnessPal good for GLP-1 users?

MyFitnessPal is excellent for macro tracking — it's the gold standard database of food items and breakdown accuracy. But it's calorie-first, not GLP-1-first. It won't warn you about high-fat or heavy foods that trigger nausea, and it'll flag your smaller portions as 'undereating' even though that's medically appropriate on semaglutide. Best used as a logging tool alongside GLP-1-specific guidance.

Do I need a special app for GLP-1, or will any calorie app work?

A calorie-only app works but isn't ideal. Standard apps assume a 2000-calorie baseline and might pressure you to eat more. GLP-1-specific guidance (like injection cycle awareness, small portion recipes, and protein-first macro targets) makes tracking less stressful and more effective. Free options like GLP Flow's resources cover those gaps without the app subscription.

What's the difference between meal planning and food logging?

Meal planning (PlateJoy, EatLove) generates weekly menus based on your preferences and macros, then sends shopping lists. Food logging (MyFitnessPal, Lose It!) tracks what you actually eat after the fact. Both are useful — planning removes decision fatigue, logging keeps you accountable. Some apps (PlateJoy) do both.

Are meal planning apps worth the subscription cost?

If you struggle with meal decisions or want structured weekly menus with shopping lists, yes — services like PlateJoy ($8-15/month) save time and reduce food waste. If you just need to log meals and track macros, free options like MyFitnessPal (free tier) or Lose It! (free tier) work fine.
